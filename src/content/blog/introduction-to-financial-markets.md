## Introduction to Financial Markets and Securities

Financial markets are systems where individuals, corporations, and governments raise capital and trade financial assets. For anyone looking to understand personal finance, learning the basic definitions of standard financial securities is the foundational starting point.

Here are the definitions of the three most common financial assets.

## 1. Equities (Stocks / Shares)

When you purchase a stock, you buy a small ownership share in a corporation.
- **Ownership:** As a shareholder, you own a fraction of the company's assets and earnings.
- **Returns:** If the company grows and increases profits, its value rises, leading to capital gains if you sell the shares. Some companies also distribute a portion of profits directly to shareholders as cash dividends.
- **Risk:** Equities are subject to market volatility; if the company underperforms or the market falls, the stock price can drop.

## 2. Debt & Fixed Income (Bonds / Deposits)

A debt security represents a loan made by an investor to a borrower (typically a corporation or government).
- **Lending:** Instead of buying ownership, you lend capital to the issuer.
- **Returns:** The issuer agrees to pay you a fixed rate of interest (coupon rate) at regular intervals and return your principal amount at the maturity date.
- **Risk:** Debt is generally safer than equities because the issuer is legally contracted to pay interest, but returns are typically lower.

## 3. Mutual Funds & ETFs

A mutual fund is a pooled investment vehicle managed by a professional asset manager.
- **Diversification:** The fund pools money from thousands of investors to buy a large portfolio of stocks, bonds, or other assets. This provides instant diversification, reducing the risk of relying on a single stock.
- **Types:** Mutual funds can be equity-based, debt-based, or hybrid (mixing both).

Most people know their salary to the dollar. They can tell you their hourly rate, their annual CTC, their take-home after tax. They track their income carefully because income feels like the scoreboard — the number that tells you how well you're doing financially.

It isn't.

Your salary tells you how much money is flowing through your hands. Your net worth tells you how much of it stayed. And the gap between those two numbers — between what most people earn over a lifetime and what they actually accumulate — is one of the most important financial stories nobody talks about honestly.

Net worth is the single most accurate measure of financial health available. Not your income. Not your credit score. Not the balance in your checking account on the day after payday. Your net worth — what you own minus what you owe — is the number that tells you whether your financial life is moving forward or standing still.

## What Net Worth Actually Is

The formula is simple:

**Net Worth = Total Assets − Total Liabilities**

Assets are everything you own that has monetary value. Liabilities are everything you owe. The difference is your net worth — positive if your assets exceed your debts, negative if they don't.

But the simplicity of the formula masks something important: **most people systematically miscalculate both sides.**

On the asset side, people tend to overcount. They include the full value of a car they still owe $18,000 on, forgetting that the loan is a liability. They include the market value of their home without subtracting the mortgage balance. They count retirement accounts at their full balance without accounting for the tax that will be due on withdrawal.

On the liability side, people tend to undercount. They forget about the remaining balance on a car lease. They ignore the interest accruing on a student loan that's been on income-based repayment for years. They overlook the credit card balance they intended to pay off last month.

An accurate net worth calculation requires honesty on both sides. The WealthMaze Net Worth Calculator walks you through each category systematically — ensuring nothing gets overlooked, overcounted, or conveniently forgotten.

## Assets: What to Include and What Not To

Not everything you own counts as an asset for net worth purposes — and some things count differently than you might expect.

**Liquid Assets — Count these in full**

These are assets you can convert to cash quickly without penalty or significant loss of value:

- Cash on hand and checking account balances
- Savings account balances
- Money market accounts
- Short-term certificates of deposit (CDs)
- Treasury bills or short-term government bonds

**Investment Assets — Count current market value**

- Stock portfolios and brokerage accounts (use today's market value, not what you paid)
- Bond holdings
- Mutual funds and ETFs
- Cryptocurrency holdings (volatile — use current value with awareness it may change significantly)
- 401(k), IRA, Roth IRA, and other retirement accounts (use current balance)

**Note on retirement accounts:** Some financial planners calculate "liquid net worth" separately, excluding retirement accounts because they cannot be accessed without penalty before age 59½. It is worth calculating both — your total net worth including retirement assets, and your liquid net worth excluding them. The gap between the two tells you how accessible your wealth actually is.

**Real Assets — Count equity, not total value**

- Your home: use estimated current market value minus the outstanding mortgage balance. Only the equity portion is your asset.
- Investment properties: same principle — market value minus remaining loan balance
- Vehicles: use current market value (tools like Kelley Blue Book for cars), then subtract any outstanding auto loan. Vehicles depreciate — a $35,000 car bought two years ago may be worth $25,000 today.

**What NOT to count:**

- Personal belongings like clothing, furniture, and everyday electronics (they have value but are impractical to liquidate and depreciate rapidly)
- Future expected income (your salary is income, not an asset — it only becomes part of net worth once you save or invest it)
- The value of your education or skills (human capital is real but not included in standard net worth calculations)

## Liabilities: The Side People Undercount

Liabilities are every financial obligation you currently owe. Be thorough here — this is where most people's net worth calculations go wrong.

**Common liabilities to include:**

- Mortgage balance (remaining principal, not monthly payment)
- Home equity loans or lines of credit (HELOC) — outstanding balance
- Auto loans — remaining balance on each vehicle
- Student loans — total outstanding balance including accrued interest
- Credit card balances — every card, even if you plan to pay it off this month
- Personal loans from banks, credit unions, or individuals
- Medical debt outstanding
- Tax debt owed to the IRS or state tax authorities
- Buy-now-pay-later balances (Affirm, Klarna, etc. — these count)
- Any other outstanding financial obligation

**The honest test:** If someone demanded payment from you today, would you have to pay this? If yes — it is a liability.

## What Your Net Worth Number Actually Means

Here is where most net worth articles stop — at the calculation. But the number itself is only useful in context.

**If your net worth is negative:**

This is more common than most people admit, particularly in your 20s and early 30s when student loans, car loans, and early career income combine to create more liabilities than assets. A negative net worth is not a crisis — it is a starting point. The question is not whether the number is negative today but whether it is improving each quarter.

According to Federal Reserve data, the median net worth for Americans under 35 is approximately $39,000. Many people in their 20s have negative or near-zero net worth. This is normal. It is not permanent unless the habits that created it are permanent.

**If your net worth is positive but small:**

Calculate the ratio of your net worth to your annual income. A rough benchmark: by age 30, a net worth of 0.5x annual income is reasonable. By 40, 2x. By 50, 4x. By 60, 7x. These are median benchmarks, not rules — but they provide useful context for whether your accumulation is on track relative to your earning years.

**If your net worth is growing:**

The direction matters more than the absolute number at any given moment. A net worth growing by $500 to $1,000 per month — through debt paydown, investment contributions, and asset appreciation — is financial progress regardless of whether the starting number is positive or negative. Track the monthly change as closely as you track the total.

## The Two Levers That Move Your Net Worth

Every financial decision either increases your net worth or decreases it. There are exactly two ways to improve it:

**1. Increase assets** — by saving money, investing consistently, and allowing existing investments to grow through compounding returns.

**2. Decrease liabilities** — by paying down debt, particularly high-interest debt that compounds against you.

These two levers interact. Paying off a credit card charging 22% annual interest is equivalent to earning a guaranteed 22% return on that money — far better than most investments in any given year. Eliminating a $10,000 credit card balance increases your net worth by $10,000 immediately, while simultaneously removing the compounding interest that was eroding it monthly.

The order of priority matters:

First, eliminate high-interest consumer debt (credit cards, personal loans above 10%). The guaranteed return of eliminating expensive debt almost always exceeds expected investment returns on a risk-adjusted basis.

Second, build an emergency fund of 3-6 months of expenses in liquid assets. Without this, any financial shock forces you into debt — destroying net worth at the worst possible moment.

Third, invest consistently into appreciating assets — index funds, retirement accounts, diversified portfolios. These grow your asset column automatically, improving net worth even in months when you make no active decisions.

Fourth, pay down moderate-rate debt (mortgages, student loans below 6-7%) over time, while continuing to invest. At these interest rates, the math often favors investing over aggressive debt paydown — but this depends on individual circumstances and risk tolerance.

## Net Worth Benchmarks by Age — A Reality Check

These figures are based on Federal Reserve Survey of Consumer Finances data and represent median net worth by age group in the United States:

| Age Group | Median Net Worth (US) | What It Means |
|-----------|----------------------|---------------|
| Under 35 | ~$39,000 | Most have student debt; early accumulation phase |
| 35–44 | ~$135,000 | Career peak approaching; real wealth building begins |
| 45–54 | ~$247,000 | Mid-accumulation; home equity and retirement assets growing |
| 55–64 | ~$364,000 | Pre-retirement; compounding accelerating |
| 65–74 | ~$410,000 | Retirement phase; drawdown beginning |

Two things to notice about this table.

First, the growth from under-35 to 65-74 is approximately 10x — driven almost entirely by consistent investing over time, not by salary increases.

Second, the median figures are modest. A 65-year-old with $410,000 in net worth, generating 4% annually in retirement income, receives approximately $16,400 per year — well below what most people need for a comfortable retirement. This is the mathematical reality of why starting early and investing consistently matters so significantly.

## The Most Important Thing Net Worth Reveals

Beyond the calculation and the benchmarks, net worth reveals something that no other financial metric does: **the relationship between your income and your wealth.**

Two people can earn identical incomes and have radically different net worths — because net worth measures not what flows through your hands but what accumulates.

Alex earns $95,000 per year. His net worth is $12,000. His income is high and his wealth is low — money is flowing through his life without building anything permanent.

Someone else earns $55,000 per year. Her net worth is $180,000 at the same age — built through consistent investing, minimal debt, and spending deliberately below her income. Her income is modest and her wealth is substantial.

The salary measures the tap. The net worth measures the tank. And the tank is the only number that determines what options you have when the tap is turned off — through retirement, job loss, illness, or any other life event that ends regular income.

Calculate your net worth today. Track it quarterly. Make every significant financial decision with one question in mind: does this increase my net worth or decrease it?

That discipline, sustained over time, is the entire strategy.

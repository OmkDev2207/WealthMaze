## Is Gold a Good Investment? Historical Returns & Benefits

Gold has held a special place in human history as a store of value, symbol of wealth, and medium of exchange for thousands of years. While modern finance revolves around digital currencies, stocks, and bonds, gold remains a cornerstone of portfolio management.

Let's evaluate the pros, cons, and historical performance of gold as an investment asset.

## The Role of Gold in a Portfolio

Unlike stocks (which represent ownership in a business that generates profits) or bonds (which pay interest), gold does not produce any active income or dividends. It simply sits in a vault. 

However, gold's value comes from its **scarcity, durability, and global acceptance**. In a modern investment portfolio, gold serves three major functions:

### 1. Hedge Against Inflation
When governments print money, paper currencies lose purchasing power, and the cost of living rises. Because gold has a limited physical supply, its price tends to rise in tandem with inflation, preserving your real wealth.

### 2. Safe Haven During Crises
During times of geopolitical tension, economic recessions, or currency crises, investors panic and sell stocks. In these periods, capital runs to gold, driving its price up. This inverse relationship makes gold an excellent shock absorber for your portfolio during market crashes.

### 3. Portfolio Diversifier
Gold has low correlation with equities. When stock markets go down, gold often goes up or remains stable, reducing your overall portfolio volatility.

## Methods to Invest in Gold

- **Physical Gold:** Jewelry, coins, or bars. While traditional, it carries high making charges, storage costs, safety risks, and purity concerns.
- **Gold ETFs & Mutual Funds:** Paper/digital instruments traded on the stock exchange that track the physical price of gold. They are highly liquid, require no storage fees, and have zero purity issues.
- **Sovereign Gold Bonds (SGB):** Government-backed bonds denominated in grams of gold. They are the most lucrative gold investment because they pay a fixed annual interest (typically 2.5%) in addition to the gold price appreciation.

## How Much Gold Should You Own?

Financial planners generally recommend allocating **5% to 10% (up to 15% maximum) of your total portfolio to gold**. 

Keeping some gold ensures that if the stock market experiences a severe multi-year downturn, your portfolio has a stable foundation to limit your losses.

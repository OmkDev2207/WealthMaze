## Market Volatility and Financial Cycles Explained

Asset prices in financial markets rarely move in a straight line. Volatility—the rate and magnitude of price changes—is a natural characteristic of equity, commodity, and bond markets. 

Understanding how volatility and financial cycles work conceptually is essential for anyone analyzing long-term investment data.

## What is Market Volatility?

Volatility is a statistical measure of the dispersion of returns for a given security or market index. 
- **High Volatility:** Prices fluctuate rapidly in either direction over a short period.
- **Low Volatility:** Prices remain relatively stable and change at a slower, more predictable pace.

Volatility is typically measured using standard deviation or index trackers like the **Volatility Index (VIX)**, which estimates near-term market risk and investor sentiment.

## The Two Main Phases of Market Cycles

Financial markets move through structural cycles, commonly referred to as bull and bear markets:

### 1. Bull Market
A bull market is a period where market prices are rising or expected to rise. It is characterized by economic growth, strong corporate profits, high employment rates, and positive investor confidence. A sustained rise of 20% or more from a recent market bottom is the standard definition of a bull market.

### 2. Bear Market
A bear market is a period of sustained price declines. It is typically defined as a drop of 20% or more from recent peaks. Bear markets are often accompanied by economic recessions, rising unemployment, and cautious investor sentiment.

## What Causes Market Volatility?

Volatility is driven by multiple macroeconomic factors:
- **Economic Data:** Announcements regarding GDP growth, unemployment statistics, and interest rate decisions by central banks.
- **Corporate Earnings:** Quarterly financial reports released by public companies.
- **Geopolitical Events:** Trade policies, international relations, and global supply chain disruptions.

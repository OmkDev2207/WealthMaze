## The Main Types of Taxes Explained

Taxes can be collected in various ways, but they are broadly divided into two major categories: **Direct Taxes** and **Indirect Taxes**. 

Understanding how these taxes work helps individuals make better sense of their receipts, income statements, and expenses.

## 1. Direct Taxes

A direct tax is levied directly on an individual or an organization's income, profit, or property. The burden of a direct tax cannot be passed on to someone else; the person who earns the income pays it directly to the government.

- **Income Tax:** Levied on the annual earnings of individuals, partnerships, and sole proprietors.
- **Corporate Tax:** Levied on the net profits earned by incorporated companies.
- **Property Tax:** Levied on real estate owners based on the estimated value of their land or buildings.
- **Capital Gains Tax:** Levied on the profits earned from selling assets like stocks, real estate, or gold.

## 2. Indirect Taxes

An indirect tax is levied on goods and services rather than income. Unlike direct taxes, the seller collects the tax from the end-consumer and passes it to the government. The consumer pays the tax indirectly as part of the purchase price.

- **Sales Tax / Value Added Tax (VAT):** A percentage tax added to the retail price of consumer items.
- **Goods and Services Tax (GST):** A unified tax system used in many countries to tax supply chains from manufacturers to consumers.
- **Customs Duty:** Levied on goods imported from other countries to regulate trade.
- **Excise Duty:** Levied on the manufacture or sale of specific goods like fuel, alcohol, or tobacco.

## Progressive vs. Regressive Taxes

- **Progressive Tax:** The tax rate increases as the taxable amount increases (e.g. Income Tax). This places a larger burden on higher-income earners.
- **Regressive Tax:** The tax rate is uniform regardless of income (e.g. Sales Tax/GST). A flat tax on bread or fuel represents a larger percentage of a low-income earner's budget than a high-income earner's budget.
